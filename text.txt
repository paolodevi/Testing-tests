Here is a structured, technically focused plan for a 2-hour internal “learning-by-training” lecture tailored to a Structural Dynamics & Aeroelasticity team, with emphasis on DLM ↔ structural grid coupling, splining, and patching. I’ll keep it pragmatic: what matters, what is done, how it’s used, and limitations at each step.


---

0. Framing the Lecture (5–10 min)

Objective

Align everyone on why coupling fidelity matters in aeroelastic workflows.

Key message

> “All aeroelastic predictions are only as good as the load/displacement transfer between aerodynamic and structural models.”



Focus points

Disconnect between:

Aerodynamic mesh (panel-based, coarse, lifting surfaces)

Structural mesh (FEM, detailed, 3D)


Coupling = energy-consistent mapping problem



---

1. Big Picture Workflow (10–15 min)

Show the full loop

1. Aerodynamic model (DLM panels)


2. Structural model (FEM grid)


3. Coupling (splines / interpolation)


4. Aeroelastic solution (flutter, response, loads)


5. Back-transfer (loads → structure, deformation → aero)



Emphasize

Bidirectional mapping:

Displacements → aerodynamic grid

Forces → structural grid



What to stress

Conservation principles:

Work/energy consistency

Force/moment equilibrium




---

2. The Aerodynamic Side: DLM (20 min)

What to explain

Basics of the Doublet Lattice Method (DLM)

Frequency-domain, linear potential flow

Panel discretization (CAERO-type thinking)

Produces:

Aerodynamic influence coefficients (AIC)

Generalized aerodynamic forces (GAF)




What matters for coupling

Aerodynamic grid = control points + panels

Degrees of freedom:

Typically vertical displacement + rotations (depending on formulation)



Limitations

Linear theory (small perturbations)

Inviscid, no separation

Frequency-domain → no strong nonlinearities

Grid is not physical structure


What to study

Roger’s approximation (for time-domain conversion)

Classic references:

Albano & Rodden (1969) – original DLM formulation

Rodden’s MSC Nastran aeroelastic theory manual




---

3. Structural Model (FEM Grid) (15 min)

What to explain

FEM representation:

Nodes, elements, DOFs (6 DOF typical)


Modal reduction:

Use of mode shapes in aeroelasticity



What matters for coupling

Structural grid is:

Dense

Physically meaningful


Often reduced:

Modal coordinates vs physical DOFs



Limitations

Modal truncation

Poor representation of local flexibility if modes are insufficient


What to study

Modal participation in aeroelastic coupling

Guyan reduction vs modal reduction



---

4. The Core Problem: Grid Mismatch (10–15 min)

This is the conceptual heart of the lecture

Problem

Aerodynamic mesh ≠ Structural mesh


Need

Two operators:

Interpolation (structure → aero)

Load transfer (aero → structure)


Key requirement

Reciprocity / energy conservation

5.2 Common Spline Types

(A) Surface Splines (Thin Plate Splines / Beam Splines)

Characteristics

Smooth interpolation

Based on minimizing strain energy


Use case

Wings, smooth lifting surfaces


Limitations

Can oversmooth

Poor for discontinuities (control surfaces, hinges)



---

(B) RBE-based Methods (Rigid Links)

Characteristics

RBE2 / RBE3-like behavior

Discrete coupling


Use case

Local attachments

Control surface hinges


Limitations

RBE2: overly stiff

RBE3: load distribution issues



---

(C) Finite Plate Splines (FPS)

Characteristics

Standard in many aeroelastic solvers

Balance between smoothness and locality


Strength

Industry standard for DLM coupling



---

(D) Beam Splines

Use case

Slender structures (wings, tails)



---

5.3 What to Emphasize

Trade-offs:

Smoothness vs locality

Accuracy vs robustness


Influence radius / patch definition

Conditioning of spline matrix



---

5.4 Limitations of Splining

Extrapolation errors (outside convex hull)

Sensitivity to:

Grid density mismatch

Poor node placement


Numerical conditioning

Non-physical deformation shapes



---

5.5 What to Study

MSC Nastran:

SPLINE1, SPLINE2, SPLINE4 entries


Key topics:

Radial basis functions (RBF)

Thin plate spline theory


Papers:

Harder & Desmarais (1972) – spline interpolation in aeroelasticity

Modern RBF coupling methods in CFD–CSM coupling

6. Patching Methods (15–20 min)

What is patching?

Instead of global splines:

Divide domain into local patches

Apply interpolation locally



---

Why use patching?

Better handling of:

Complex geometries

Discontinuities

Control surfaces




---

Techniques

Substructuring

Local spline regions

Mortar methods (advanced)



---

Limitations

Interface inconsistencies

Patch boundary artifacts

Requires manual setup



---

What to study

Domain decomposition methods

Mortar FEM coupling (for advanced understanding)

Industrial practices (Airbus/Boeing style workflows if accessible internally)
